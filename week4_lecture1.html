Week 4: Research Design & Causality

Start with a judgment

Participants in a job training program have higher employment rates.
The program works
There is a difference, but causation is unclear
Most people initially pick “the program works.” That response is intuitive—but it assumes that the program caused the outcome. The key Week 4 skill is slowing down and asking what else could explain the difference.

What could go wrong?

Before seeing any explanation, list a possible alternative cause.

Show possible explanations
Motivation, prior experience, and education may influence both program participation and employment. These are confounding variables.
Motivation → Program Participation
Motivation → Employment

Design determines credibility

Observational study
True experiment
A true experiment uses random assignment, which helps balance groups before treatment. Observational studies do not, so alternative explanations remain plausible.

Test your intuition

Volunteers choose the program
Participants are randomly assigned
Random assignment distributes characteristics like motivation across groups, making them more comparable. This strengthens causal inference.

Apply before explanation

Scores improved due to maturation
The program caused improvement
Students naturally improve over time. Without a strong design, we cannot attribute improvement to the program.

Final check

Before accepting a policy claim, what should you ask first?

What research design produced this evidence?
Are the numbers large?
